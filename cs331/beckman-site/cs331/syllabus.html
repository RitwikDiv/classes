Course Objectives

  • Explain, implement, and apply the following data-structures:

    • lists (unordered and ordered),
    • stacks,
    • queues,
    • expression trees,
    • binary search trees,
    • up-trees,
    • hash tables, and
    • heaps.

  • Analyze the time and space complexity of algorithms using asymptotic upper bounds (big-\(\cal{O}\) notation).

  • Explain and use references and linked structures.

  • Outline basic object-oriented design concepts: composition, inheritance, polymorphism.

  • Outline basic concepts of immutable data structures and explain the trade-offs compared to mutable data.

  • Write and test recursive procedures, and explain the run-time stack concept.

  • Analyze searching and sorting algorithms, and explain their relationship to data-structures.

  • Choose and implement appropriate data-structures to solve an application problem.

  • Understand techniques of software development, such as unit testing and version control.

Assignments

Typically there will be between six and eight programming assignments. They will be released according to the lab schedule.

There will also be in-class assignments / activities. These will not be graded, but will be checked for adequate participation. These activities collectively will be worth 10% of your final grade. They are expected to be done in class, but will be accepted by the beginning of the next class.

Note that the particulars of the assignments (such as due dates, and even number) may change as course circumstances dictate.

The labs, regardless of number, are collectively worth 15% of the course grade.

Late assignments are not accepted under any circumstances. If you have a legitimate reason for missing an assignment, it is possible to have it excused. Occasionally the instructor may extend the due date of an assignment for the entire class, but this is rare.

Exams

We will have two midterms (Friday, February 27 and Friday, April 3) and one final exam (Date TBD). They are each worth 25%.

The final exam is cumulative. If the final exam score is better than a particular midterm score, then the final exam will replace your midterm score as well. So if you get 50% on exam 1, a 75% on exam 2, and 70% on the final, we will grade it as if you got 70% on exam 1. But, if you got 70% on each midterm and 50% on the final, then the scores stand as-is.

We can implement the algorithm in Clojure like this:

(def exam-1-score (max exam-1-score final-exam-score))
(def exam-2-score (max exam-2-score final-exam-score))
