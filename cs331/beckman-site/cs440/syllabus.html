Course Objectives

The purpose of this course is to give you a working knowledge of the fundamentals of programming languages. Programming languages are mathematical objects, and implementations of different models of computation. By the end of the course, you will understand these models and how to turn them into languages.

  • Compare the major classes of programming languages, describing their techniques, features, and styles.
  • Demonstrate how to select an apporpriate programming language for a particular task.
  • Read a formal specification of a language and implement it.
  • Use four powerful ideas:
    • Recursion
    • Abstraction
    • Transformation
    • Unification

The emphasis is on learning the theory, and using it in an implementation.

Assignments

Typically there will be between six and eight programming assignments, called machine problems (MPs). They will be released approximately one every two weeks.

There will also be in-class assignments / activities. These will not be graded, but will be checked for adequate participation. These activities collectively will be worth 10% of your final grade. They are expected to be done in class, but will be accepted by the beginning of class one week later.

Note that the particulars of the assignments (such as due dates, and even number) may change as course circumstances dictate.

The MPs, regardless of number, are collectively worth 20% of the course grade.

Late assignments are not accepted under any circumstances. If you have a legitimate reason for missing an assignment, it is possible to have it excused. Occasionally the instructor may extend the due date of an assignment for the entire class, but this is rare.

Exams

We will have one midterm (Thursday, June 25) and one final exam (Thursday, July 23). They are each worth 25%.

The final exam is cumulative. If the final exam score is better than a particular midterm score, then the final exam will replace your midterm score as well. So if you get 50% on exam 1, a 75% on exam 2, and 70% on the final, we will grade it as if you got 70% on exam 1. But, if you got 70% on each midterm and 50% on the final, then the scores stand as-is.

We can implement the algorithm in Haskell like this:

exam_1_score = max exam_1 final_exam exam_2_score = max exam_2 final_exam
